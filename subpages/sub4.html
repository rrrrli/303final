Intimate exchange over time for boy and girls






Intimate exchange is the action of sharing information that is private, secret, or emotional with a friend. Adolescents in intimate friendships tend to show higher social competency and emotional well-being. Girls are more likely to self-disclose emotions and seek emotional support than boys, which translates into higher levels of intimate exchange in friendships. One study has also found that late adolescent boys are likely to lose the intimate friendships they built in early and mid-adolescence, perhaps due to their increasing awareness and adherence to the stereotypical standards of male friendships that regard intimacy as feminine.

Similar to what past research has found, girls tended to report more frequent intimate exchange with close friends than boys. Most girls reported medium-high to high levels of intimate exchange. For boys, it seems that levels of intimate exchange varied widely between individuals at T1 from as low as scores of 3 to 5 (17%) to higher scores of 12 and 13 (21%). This pattern persisted at T2, although now boys most commonly reported scores between 8-12 (around 40%). At T3, the distribution seems slightly bimodal, with a cluster of boys reporting scores of 7 and 8 (around 23%) and another cluster reporting scores of 11 and 12 (around 29%). These interesting changes in the distribution of boys’ friendship intimacy call for more detailed examination in research.
